THE EARLY LIFE OF RIZAL

Rizal's first breathe

June 19, 1861

JOSE RIZAL, the seventh child of Francisco Mercado Rizal and Teodora Alonso y Quintos, was born in Calamba, Laguna.

Baptism

June 22, 1861

He was baptized JOSE RIZAL MERCADO at the Catholic of Calamba by the parish priest Rev. Rufino Collantes with Rev. Pedro Casañas as the sponsor.

Burnt records

September 28, 1862

The parochial church of Calamba and the canonical books, including the book in which Rizal’s baptismal records were entered, were burned.

New learnings

1864

Barely three years old, Rizal learned the alphabet from his mother.

Departed Soul

1864

When he was four years old, his sister Conception, the eight child in the Rizal family, died at the age of three. It was on this occasion that Rizal remembered having shed real tears for the first time.

Love for education

1865 – 1867

In his childhood, Rizal learned reading and writing from his mother and studied Latin with Leon Monroy until Monroy's death. During this period, two of his mother's cousins visited Calamba. Uncle Manuel Alberto encouraged Rizal's love for nature, while Uncle Gregorio emphasized the importance of education, advising him to work hard, think independently, and visualize tasks.

Pilgrimage to Antipolo

June 5, 1868

Rizal and his father fulfilled his mother's vow by journeying to the Shrine of the Virgin of Antipolo after surviving a difficult childbirth. They then visited Rizal's sister, Saturnina, who was studying at La Concordia College in Manila.

First poem

1869

At the age of eight, Rizal wrote his first poem entitled "Sa Aking Mga Kabata." The poem was written in tagalog and had for its theme "Love of One’s Language."

Home Education to Formal Education (1861 - 1870)

Jose Rizal's first instructor was his mother named Teodora Alonso Realonda; she taught him to read, and pray, and motivated him to compose poetry. He was also later taught Spanish and Latin by private tutors. After that, he was assigned to a private school in Biñan which was the start of his formal education.

Ateneo Municipal de Manila (1872 - 1877)

Rizal began his secondary schooling at the Ateneo Municipal de Manila when he was 11 years old. In courses like philosophy,physics, chemistry, and natural history, he received top grades. He read books,composed poetry that won awards, and even a play called "Junto al Pasig" while attending this school. He also developed his longtime hobbies in drawing painting and clay modeling

University of Santo Tomas (1877 - 1878)

Rizal eventually graduated from the Ateneo Municipal with a degree in land surveying and appraisal while studying philosophy and letters at the University of SantoTomas, which he also took medicine in 1878.

Universidad Central de Madrid (1882 - 1884)

He also went to Europe to study for Licentiate in Philosophy and Letters and Opthalmology at Universidad Central de Madrid.After finishing his medical course hestarted traveling to European and Asiancountries. The first country he visited atthat time was Spain followed by Singapore,Ceylon (currently known as Sri Lanka),Italy, France, Barcelona in Spain, Paris inFrance, Madrid in Spain, and so on.

University of Heidelberg (1885 - 1887)

In the Universidad Central de Madrid, where he also studied philosophy and literature, Rizal obtained a Licentiate in Medicine. He had the idea for Noli MeTangere while he was in Madrid. He also studied at the University of Paris and finished his eye-specific training at the University of Heidelberg in 1887. Rizal's publication of his first book in Berlin also occurred in that year as well,

Rizal's Return: A Journey of Healing, Education, and Historical Inquiry

1887 - 1888

To pursue his profession, Rizal returned to the Philippines. He was eager to treat his mother's eyes while also treating young Filipinos and introducing European culture. Afterward, on another trip to Europe, he went to London to conduct historical research on the Philippines.

THE LOVE LIFE OF RIZAL

Segunda Katigbak

Segunda Katigbak was her puppy love and with her the Rizal was believed to have had love at first sight. In his ‘Memorias de Un Estudiante de Manila’, Rizal graphically described her as a short lady with eloquent eyes, rosy cheeks, and smile that reveals very beautiful teeth. Unfortunately, his first love was engaged and to be married to a town mate Manuel Luz.

Leonor Valenzuela

Leonor Valenzuela, a tall girl from Pagsanjan. Rizal send her love notes written in invisible ink, that could only be deciphered over the warmth of the lamp or candle. He visited her on the eve of his departure to Spain and bade her a last goodbye.

Leonor Rivera

Leonor Rivera, Rizal's 11-year sweetheart, strongly influenced him against falling for other women while he traveled. Despite their bond, her mother disapproved due to Rizal's political activities, hiding his letters from Leonor. Believing he had moved on, Leonor consented to marry Henry Kipping, her mother's choice.

Consuelo Ortiga

Consuelo Ortiga y Rey, daughter of Don Pablo Ortiga, loved Rizal. He wrote her a poem but backed off to stay faithful to Leonor Rivera and maintain his friendship with Eduardo de Lete, who also loved Consuelo.

O Sei San

O Sei San, a Japanese samurai’s daughter taught Rizal the Japanese art of painting known as su-mie. She also helped Rizal improve his knowledge of Japanese language. If Rizal was a man without a patriotic mission, he would have married this lovely and intelligent woman and lived a stable and happy life with her in Japan because Spanish legation there offered him a lucrative job.

Gertrude Beckett

Rizal lodged with the Beckett family in London. Gertrude, the eldest daughter, fell for him. Tottie, another daughter, assisted him artistically. To evade Gertrude's affection, Rizal left for Paris, leaving behind a carved group of the Beckett sisters as a memento of their brief connection.

Nellie Boustead

After losing Leonor Rivera, Rizal found interest in Nellie, one of Eduardo Boustead's daughters. Despite Antonio Luna's advances towards her, Rizal dueled Luna over his comments about Nellie. However, they didn't marry due to religious and financial issues. They remained friends as Rizal left Europe.

Suzanne Jacoby

In 1890, Rizal moved to Brussels because of the high cost of living in Paris. In Brussels, he lived in the boarding house of the two Jacoby sisters. In time, they fell deeply in love with each other. Suzanne cried when Rizal left Brussels and wrote him when he was in Madrid.

Josephine Bracken

In late February 1895, while in Dapitan, Rizal met Josephine Bracken, an 18-year-old Irish girl seeking treatment for her adopted father's blindness. Despite opposition from Rizal's sisters and the church, they married without the church's blessing. Sadly, Josephine later gave birth prematurely to a stillborn baby, possibly due to a shocking incident.

THE WRITINGS OF RIZAL

NOVELS & ESSAY

POETRY
Noli Me Tángere
1887

Written in Spanish and published in February 1887, the title “Noli Me tangere” a Latin means “Touch Me Not”. Rizal wrote the novel as a reflection of the experiences of the Filipino during the Spanish colonization. He wants to open the eyes of Filipino that they were being oppressed, abused and accused by the friars.

El Filibusterismo
1891

Written in Spanish and the title means “the Reign of the Greed” it is the second novel written by Jose Rizal as sequel to Noli Me Tangere. Rizal dedicated the novel to the three martyred priests of Cavity mutiny and played a significant to the title of the novel.

Makamisa
1892

Makamisa is the unfinished novel written by Jose Rizal, this novel believes to be the sequel of El Filibusterismo. it was later on discovered by the historian Ambeth Ocampo in 1987, were Ocampo reconstruct the draft and now known “Makamisa” means “After mass”.

Alin Mang Lahi (“Whate’er the Race”)
1884

Alin Mang Lahi is a song composed by Jose Rizal in 1884. Rizal wrote this song to tell about how we Filipinos have a long search for freedom because of colonization and our willingness to fight for the freedom we seek.

Kundiman
1891

Jose Rizal wrote the “Kundiman” in Tagalog in September 12th, 1891. Kundiman was use to sang by young Filipino to serenade the woman they love. This traditional song wrote by Jose Rizal is about the love for his Motherland and he believes Philippines would be freed from injustice and bondage.

Brindis Speech: A Toast Honoring Juan Luna and Felix Resurreccion Hidalgo
1884

Jose Rizal gave a toast to honor Juan Luna and Felix Resurreccion Hidalgo in 1884. The two won a gold and silver medal at the National Exhibition of Fine Arts in Madrid. In Rizal speech he made clear that the victory of Luna and Hidalgo brought pride in the Philippines and their work are thoroughly inspiring and motivating.

Noli Me Rizal’s Letters is a compendium of Dr. Jose Rizal’s letters to his family members, Blumentritt, Fr. Pablo Pastells and other reformers “Come se gobiernan las Filipinas” (Governing the Philippine islands)
1888
Filipinas dentro de cien años essay
1889-1890

Filipinas dentro de cien años essay was written by Jose Rizal this essay meant to forecast the future of the Philippines and how Filipino will suffer under the Spaniards. The essay also promote nationalism among Filipino, to remind them to fight for their rights.

La Indolencia de los Filipinos, essay
1890

La Indolencia de los Filipinos is an essay written by Jose Rizal, He wrote this essay to explain the alleged idleness of Filipino during the Spanish colonization. He stated that Filipino are industrial  and hardworking before the Spanish colonization.  

Sa Mga Kababaihang Taga Malolos, essay
1889

Jose Rizal wrote the “Sa mga kababaihan Taga Malolos” in 1889, were Rizal dedicated the letter to woman hailing from malolos. Rizal was expressed admiration for the women from Malolos due to their proactive pursuit of education and self-improvement. He also belief with regards to significance of education is for both gender.

Annotations to Antonio de Moragas, Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas, essay
1889

Rizal do a research to seek the truth about the Philippines before it been colonized by Spanish. He also compared his research to the rare book of Antonio de Moragas to know the truth about the society and culture of the Philippines.

A La Juventud Filipina
1879

At eighteen years of age, Rizal won first prize for his poem “To the Philippine Youth” in 1879 in a poetry contest organized for Filipino poets by the Manila Lyceum of Art and Literature. This poem is one of his most famous poems.

El Canto Del Viajero (English translation: Song of the Wanderer)
1895

Song of the Wanderer is a poem that talks about Jose Rizal himself as a wanderer or traveler. In this poem, Rizal describes the loneliness of being a wanderer (reflecting on his return to the Philippines from Europe) and, after being away from his homeland for a long time, the heartbreak of being a stranger in the land of his birth.

Canto de María Clara (English translation: Song of Maria Clara)
1887

Maria Clara is a character in Rizal’s novel, Noli Me Tangere. This poem, Song of Maria Clara, is part of the novel sung by Maria Clara.

Himno Al Trabajo (Dalit sa Paggawa)
1888

The poem “Himno al Trabajo,” written by Jose Rizal. Rizal dedicated his poem to the people of Lipa, Batangas. He stated in his poem that the hardworking people of Lipa. The poem is later sung to celebrate Lipa being a city.

Felicitación
1875

Felicitación (felicitation or congratulation in English) was written by Jose Rizal in 1875 to greet his brother-in-law, Antonio Lopez on his saint’s day. Rizal was fourteen years old. For the English version of this poem, see Felicitation.

Kundiman
1891

A kundiman is a traditional Filipino love song sang by a young man to serenade the woman of his love. The theme of Rizal’s “Kundiman” was his intense love for his Motherland. His words reflected his optimism that the Philippines would be freed from injustice and bondage. 

Me Piden Versos (English translation: They Ask Me for Verses!)
1882

Rizal’s “Me Piden Versos” was written while he was a member of Circulo Hispano Filipino. Me Piden Versos (They Asked Me for Verses) reflects how sad it was for him to have the ability to steer emotions through his poems, and have these emotions stifled and muted by the powerful and oppressive Spaniards.

Mi Primera Inspiracion
1874

Mi Primera Inspiracion (My First Inspiration) is a Spanish poem that is believed to have been written by Jose Rizal when he was nine years old. There are claims, however, that this poem was actually written by his nephew, Antonio Lopez-Rizal (Narcisa’s son) because of the seeming likeness in handwriting of the two Rizals.

Mi Retiro (English translation: My Retreat)
1896

My Last Farewell (Mi Ultimo Adios in Spanish) was the last poem that Jose Rizal wrote. It had no title when Rizal finished writing it. It was Mariano Ponce, his friend, who gave the title to this poem.

Por La Educación (Recibe Lustre La Patria)
1876

Rizal wrote the poem ‘Por La Educación (Recibe Lustre La Patria)’ to highlight the importance of education to the country and its citizens.

Sa Sanggol na si Jesus (English translation: Child Jesus)
1875

“Child Jesus” was written in Spanish by Jose Rizal in 1875 at age of 14. The poem is an octave real or a short poem with eight verses only which was actually according to the Spanish standard of writing poems. Like “To the Virgin Mary“, this is another religious poem by Rizal.

A Mi Musa
1890

Rizal wrote A Mi Musa because of disappointment over the half- hearted attitude of the Filipino expatriates working for the reforms in Spain. And his poem expressed his bitterness over the troubles in Calamba.

Un Recuerdo A Mi Pueblo
1892

In this nostalgic poem, Jose Rizal remembers his childhood days in Calamba, Laguna. Rizal had the happiest and most beautiful memories of the place, the hospitality and friendliness as well as the industry of the people of Calamba. Those memories were influential in molding his character and his values.

Mi Ultimo Adiós (English translation: My Last Farewell)
1896

My Last Farewell (Mi Ultimo Adios in Spanish) was the last poem that Jose Rizal wrote. It had no title when Rizal finished writing it. It was Mariano Ponce, his friend, who gave the title to this poem.

THE LEGACY OF RIZAL

To the Philippines

Jose Rizal's legacy continues to shape the future of the Philippines in various ways, including its socio-political landscape, cultural identity, and educational system. His ideas of nationalism, social justice, and the pursuit of knowledge remain relevant as the country strives for progress and development.

To Philippine Education

Jose Rizal made significant contributions to education, particularly in advocating for educational reforms during the Spanish colonial era in the Philippines. His ideas emphasized the importance of education in liberating the Filipino people from ignorance and oppression.

To the Philippines as a Nation

He is revered as a national hero and his works, writings, and ideas continue to shape Filipino identity and culture. Rizal's emphasis on nationalism, social justice, and the pursuit of knowledge has left an indelible mark on various aspects of Filipino culture, including literature, art, politics, and education.

To the Philippine Politics

His writings and ideas played a crucial role in inspiring the Filipino people to fight for independence and political reforms. Rizal advocated for reforms within the Spanish colonial system and called for greater representation and rights for Filipinos. His nationalist views and emphasis on the importance of self-governance continue to influence political discourse in the Philippines.
